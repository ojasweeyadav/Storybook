The Day the Zoo Went on Strike

By: Rhea Malhotra

At the edge of the bustling city stood Maplewood Zoo, a cozy place full of giggles, camera clicks, and the occasional flying popcorn kernel. Children loved it, parents tolerated it, and animals—well, they were getting tired.

“I’m telling you, Max,” grumbled Greta the giraffe, “I cannot stretch my neck one more time for these selfies.”

Max the monkey swung down from a tree. “Yesterday a boy tried to feed me a banana peel. Just the peel! What kind of insult is that?”

“They banged on my glass again,” sighed Terry the turtle. “I’m already slow. Do I need trauma too?”

The animals gathered under the old fig tree by the lion enclosure. Leo, the lion king himself, stretched lazily and cleared his throat. “Friends, I believe it’s time for change.”

“Change?” whispered Penny the penguin. “Like a new zookeeper?”

Leo shook his mane. “No. A strike.”

The word bounced through the zoo like a rogue beach ball.

“A STRIKE?” gasped Greta. “You mean we stop doing… animal things?”

“Exactly,” said Leo. “No roars, no flips, no posing. Just sit still. Let the humans wonder.”

The idea was wild. Ridiculous. Brilliant.

By morning, it began.

Max refused to swing or shriek. Greta tucked her neck like a moody accordion. Terry hid in his shell. Penny waddled backward. Even Leo lay with his back turned to the crowd.

The visitors were baffled. “Why aren’t the animals doing anything?” a boy whined.

“Maybe they’re tired,” said his mother.

By noon, crowds gathered, expecting some dramatic show. Instead, they got silence, slouched animals, and zero entertainment. Panic spread among the zookeepers.

“Are they sick?” asked Jerry the head keeper.

“No,” whispered a little girl watching through the rail. “They’re sad.”

Word traveled fast. A reporter caught wind and wrote: *‘Maplewood Zoo Animals Refuse to Perform. Are They Unhappy?’*

It went viral.

That night, under the moonlight, the animals held a second meeting.

“It’s working,” said Max, nibbling a grape.

“Tomorrow, we talk,” said Leo.

The next morning, a large sign hung over the lion’s den. It read in big bold letters: “WE WANT RESPECT, REST, AND BETTER SNACKS.”

Below that, in smaller pawprints: “And no tapping on the glass. Seriously.”

The humans were stunned. Children stared, adults took photos, and the mayor showed up personally.

By evening, a city meeting was held. “If animals are on strike, what does that say about us?” asked the mayor. “We need to listen.”

Changes came fast. More green space. Nap hours. Better food. Glass with signs that said “Please do not tap—we’re watching you.”

The next day, Max did a backflip. Greta lifted her neck proudly. Terry did a slow, heroic crawl. Penny slid down an ice ramp just for fun. Leo roared—a deep, satisfied sound.

The crowd cheered. But now, they also clapped softly. They waved, whispered, and thanked the animals with smiles, not noise.

The zoo was never the same.

Every Sunday, the animals got a “Lazy Day” with hammocks and music. And a statue was built outside the gates: A bronze monkey holding a tiny sign that read: “Even the smallest voice can start the loudest roar.”

And that, dear reader, is how the zoo went on strike… and made the world listen.
